Usability Evaluation of Georgia State University

Jane, Jeffrey, and Kenna

COGS 187A

2. Match Between System and the Real World

"The system should speak the users' language, with words, phrases and concepts familiar to the user, rather than system-oriented terms. Follow real-world conventions, making information appear in a natural and logical order."
– Jakob Nielsen


Under the "About" section in the bottom navigation of the website, there are categories that seem irrelevant to the section title. The overall title could be something that is more meaningful to the subcategories underneath, or the categories could be distributed to other areas on the website.



If the user happens to not attend Georgia State University, they may not understand what the “Rialto Center” is. When clicking on the link, the user is taken to another site where then it becomes more clear that it’s a name for their Arts Center. Instead of putting “Rialto” on their homepage, they could write out “Arts Center” so that people will generally know where to look for the Arts Center and maybe once the user goes on that page, they could title it “Rialto Center” for users who do not attend Georgia State University.



When going to “About,” it leads you to a page where it talks of “About Georgia State University” but there, it contains a left sidebar that is also titled “About,” and one of the subcategories is “Points of Distinction,” which is very vague and broad. Once you click on it, you realize it’s just “quick facts” about what GSU is most proud of. I thought this kind of information is not exactly necessary and should not be in their “About” section.



The website does a fair job in communicating its information without alienating the user base. For instance, going to the “Global Engagement” section from the navigation bar, we would expect to see a lot of different organizations and ambiguous topics due to the nature of the topic. However, GSU sets it up so they include both the fully expanded name of the subject along with the acronym in parentheses. We see FIPE Grants which are also known as Faculty International Partnership Engagement Grants. We also see URSA in this section. This serves to educate the user about possibly future phrases that might become more familiar, but also gives them the full on meaning upfront. This does not compromise the functionality or look of the website either.
